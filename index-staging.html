How Much Does a Trip to Bali Cost in 2026?

Destination: Bali, Indonesia Currency: Indonesian Rupiah (IDR) · prices shown in USD Last verified: April 2026

Bali has a reputation for being cheap. That reputation is about 60% accurate. If you land in Seminyak, eat at Instagram restaurants, and book your activities through the hotel, you will spend European city-break money. If you're willing to rent a scooter, eat where the Balinese eat, and stay slightly off the main strip, the island is still genuinely affordable.

The honest version: budget travelers can live comfortably on $35–50/day. Mid-range — private villa, decent restaurants, one or two day trips — is $80–150/day. The "I deserve this" version with spa mornings and rice-paddy infinity pools runs $200+/day. All three are real and achievable.

Ubud and Canggu have the best guesthouse-to-price ratios. A clean private room with fan and breakfast in Ubud runs 150,000–250,000 IDR ($9–15). Skip the AC if you can — you're often paying double just for that.

Food: a plate of nasi goreng at a warung costs $1–2. The same dish at a Seminyak restaurant is $8–12. This is the single biggest budget lever you have. Eat two meals at warungs, treat yourself to one restaurant dinner, and you'll easily stay under $10/day on food. Markets like Pasar Badung in Denpasar are where locals shop — cheap fresh fruit, snacks, and cooked food.

Getting around: rent a scooter for 70,000–100,000 IDR/day ($4–6). Fuel is cheap — you'll spend maybe $1 extra per day. If you're not confident on a scooter, Gojek (the Indonesian Grab) is inexpensive for shorter trips. Don't take a taxi from the airport without agreeing on a fixed price upfront — the metered rate often doesn't apply.

Activities: temple entry costs 30,000–50,000 IDR ($2–3) plus sarong rental if you don't bring your own. Tegallalang Rice Terrace has a small entrance fee. A lot of the best things in Bali — beaches, sunsets at Tanah Lot, sunrise hikes — cost nothing or close to it.

This is where Bali really delivers. For $40–65/night you get a private villa with a small plunge pool — the kind of accommodation that would cost $300+ in Europe. Areas like Ubud, Seminyak, and Canggu have dozens of options in this range. Book direct or through Booking.com rather than through a tour operator and you'll typically save 15–20%.

Food: a proper sit-down lunch at a decent Ubud restaurant — say, a smoothie bowl, a main, and a fresh juice — runs $8–14. Dinner at somewhere with a view and a cocktail is $20–30. There's no reason to eat badly at this budget level. Bali's cafe scene, particularly in Canggu and Ubud, is genuinely good.

Transport: keep the scooter for local exploring, and book a shared shuttle or private car for day trips. Perama runs comfortable tourist shuttles between the main areas for $8–15. A private car and driver for a full day (including fuel and parking) costs $45–70 — excellent value if you're splitting with a travel companion.

Activities: a cooking class in Ubud is $30–45 and includes a market visit. White water rafting on the Ayung River is $30–40. A 60-minute traditional Balinese massage at a reputable spa is $12–20. Budget in $20–30/day for activities and you'll have no shortage of things to do.

Bali's top end is legitimate world-class and still cheaper than equivalent hotels in Thailand or the Maldives. COMO Shambhala, Alila Ubud, and Capella Ubud are the standard references — but there are also dozens of private villas with staff that run $200–600/night for the whole property, not per room.

Food: Locavore in Ubud is a Michelin-level tasting menu for $80–100/person — genuinely one of the best restaurants in Southeast Asia. Sundara at Four Seasons, Metis in Seminyak — you're looking at $60–100/person with drinks. Worth it occasionally; not worth it every night when a $15 dinner two streets away is almost as good.

Transport: a dedicated driver for the duration of your stay is the standard at this level. Expect to pay $60–80/day for a driver who knows the island. They'll get you to Pura Lempuyang for sunrise without you navigating in the dark, which alone is worth the cost.

Activities: private surf lessons are $60–80/session. A half-day private rafting tour with lunch is $100+. Spa days at hotel properties start around $80 for a two-hour treatment package. The Mount Batur sunrise trek with a guide runs $40–60 — one of the better value experiences at any budget level.

Hidden Costs People Forget

These don't show up in most budget calculators but they add up, especially on shorter trips.

  • $35 Visa on arrival (VOA) — required for most nationalities. Paid on arrival at Ngurah Rai airport. There's now also a tourist levy of around $10 introduced in 2024 that's sometimes bundled with the VOA, sometimes collected separately.
  • $10–15 Tourist tax (Bali tourism levy) — formally introduced in February 2024, 150,000 IDR per international visitor. Collected separately from the visa. You can pay online before arrival at lovebali.id or at the airport.
  • $15–25 Scooter deposit — most rental shops ask for your passport as collateral or a cash deposit. Never leave your actual passport. Pay the deposit, or rent from a reputable shop that doesn't demand it.
  • $3–5 ATM fees per withdrawal — Balinese ATMs charge 50,000–75,000 IDR per transaction regardless of amount. Withdraw larger amounts less frequently. Some machines also have low withdrawal limits (1,500,000 IDR ≈ $92), so check before queuing.
  • $1–3 Sarong rental at temples — compulsory at most Hindu temples if you're not wearing one. Some charge for the sarong, some lend them free. Bring your own from a market (under $3) and you won't have to think about it.
  • $30–60 Airport transfers — metered taxis from Ngurah Rai are officially around $10–20 to Seminyak or Kuta, but airport touts charge more. Pre-book through your hotel or use the official taxi counter inside arrivals. Return trips are cheaper if arranged in advance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Bali cheap for tourists?
Bali is cheap by Western standards but prices have risen noticeably since 2022. A budget traveler spending mindfully can get by on $35–50/day. Mid-range comfort — a private pool villa, sit-down meals, one or two paid activities — runs $80–150/day. The island is still good value, but the era of $20/day backpacking is mostly over in the popular areas.
How much spending money per day in Bali?
Budget $35–50/day if you're staying in guesthouses and eating local. Allow $80–150/day for mid-range — a private room with AC, restaurant meals, and scooter hire. Comfortable travelers spending on villa stays, tours, and spa days typically land at $150–250/day. These figures exclude flights and the visa on arrival ($35 USD).
Is $1,000 enough for 2 weeks in Bali?
Yes — $1,000 for 14 days works out to about $71/day, which is achievable at the mid-budget level if you're disciplined. Stay in guesthouses or budget villas ($25–40/night), eat at warungs for most meals, rent a scooter ($5–7/day), and limit paid activities to one or two highlights. You won't be roughing it, but you will need to skip the $25 sunset cocktails most nights.
What is the cheapest time to visit Bali?
The wet season (November to March) brings lower accommodation prices — sometimes 30–40% less than peak rates. January and February are the wettest months. If you don't mind afternoon rain and want cheaper flights and quieter temples, shoulder months like October or April hit the sweet spot: decent weather, no holiday crowds, and prices that haven't spiked yet.
How much does a week in Bali cost for 2 people?
A week for two people typically costs $700–2,100 total on the ground (excluding flights), depending on budget level. Budget: around $700–900 (sharing a guesthouse, eating local). Mid-range: $1,100–1,600 (private villa, mix of restaurants). Comfort: $1,800–3,500+ (boutique resort, guided tours, spa days). Add $35 each for the visa on arrival and $10 each for the tourist levy.
